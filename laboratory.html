Cell Staining & Imaging

In this experiment, I observed nuclei morphology and muscle filament of mammalian cells using fluorescence microscopy. Following proper aseptic technique, I prepared the cell culture samples by washing with phosphate buffered saline (PBS) and plated into an IBIDI imaging chamber. Then to preserve the morphology and structure of the cells I incubated with paraformaldehyde to create crosslink and protein bridges with free amine groups and amino acid side chains. After fixing the cell, I permeabilized the cell membrane using Triton X-100 to allow fluorescent labeling agents to bind with intracellular structures. I also added bovine serum albumin (BSA) protein to prevent nonspecific binding. Finally, I stained the cell using phalloidin, Alexa-Fluor-488 and Hoechst dye. Each of these compounds binds to specific structures in the cell such as F-actin and the cell nuclei. This experiment taught me a lot about flurophores and fluorescence imaging where these chemical compounds can be joined to specific antibodies/proteins to detect targeted antigens in cells. A fluorescence microscope can then be used to excite these compounds causing them to emit light allowing the camera to detect photon emission and wavelength patterns to produce these images.

Features: Fluorescence Microscopy, Image Processing, Cell Imaging, Fiji (ImageJ), Aseptic Technique, Micro Pipetting, Cell Culture

DNA Manipulation

In this experiment, I performed gel electrophoresis to separate DNA fragments based on size. I started off by learning about plasmid DNA which can be used as vectors to deliver genetic material into cells as well as synthetically constructed to manipulate or amplify DNA sequences. This lab takes advantage of the compact nature and their self-replicating properties. I prepared the DNA by applying restriction enzymes to cut the plasmid at specific regions, creating different DNA fragments. To cut the DNA I used a restriction digestion buffer which contains NaCl, Tris-HCL, and Mg2+ ions to create an optimal environment for the enzyme to function. Taking advantage of the negative charge of DNA, I set up an agarose gel electrophoresis system to separate the DNA fragments based on size. A buffer solution was used to provide conductance for the electric field to be generated along the gel. An application of gel electrophoresis is to help separate desired DNA fragments which can be isolated for cloning using restriction enzymes to cut the complete DNA and DNA ligase being used to bind the isolated pieces together. Then this plasmid can be introduced into a host cell, usually bacteria like E. coli to efficiently replicate the DNA.

Features: Gel Electrophoresis, DNA Manipulation, Restriction Enzymes, Agarose Gel, DNA Ladder, Micro Pipetting

Protein Engineering

This case study involved osteoporosis and the reaction mechanism of human aromatase enzyme which catalyzes the reaction converting testosterone and androstendedione to estradiol and estrone. When there is aberrant activity, it reduces estrogen production and with low estrogen levels it leads to weakened bone structure resulting in osteoporosis. This experiment explores manipulating genetic sequences to optimize reaction kinetics. I first studied the reaction mechanism and active site, identifying 3 important residues in the binding cavity. The next step was to individually select and mutate those amino acid sequences to alter reactivity. The two focal points for a successful mutation is to observe the structural and chemical compatibility. For structure, I avoided steric hindrances and aimed for bulkier, aromatic side chains that brought the amino acid residue closer, reducing the pocket size. I also needed to make sure the mutated sequence is chemically consistent where if the target molecule is hydrophobic, the mutated residue should also have the same properties. Once these criteria are fulfilled I altered the FASTA sequence on Swiss-Model to generate a mutated model of the aromatase enzyme. Then I docked the ligands into the active site of the mutant model to estimate the Gibbs free energy of binding to determine the feasibility of the mutation.

Features: Molecular Modeling, Active Site Analysis, PyMOL, Swiss-Model, CB-Dock 2

Protein Release Kinetics

I investigated the release kinetics of proteins from hydrogel spheres by immersing the spheres in buffer and collecting samples at specific time intervals. Protein concentration for each sample was quantified using a bicinchoninic acid (BCA) protein assay with variations in colour indicating different concentration levels. The absorbance was then measured using a spectrophotometer and compared to a standard curve generated from known concentrations. This helped me construct release profiles over time to determine diffusion rates and total protein release for each sample.

Features: BCA Protein Assay, Spectrophotometry, Chromatography, Reaction Kinetics
